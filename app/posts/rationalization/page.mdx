## Rationalization - Notes from MM-110

When there is a square root in quotients, it is very common to rewrite the quotient in a form where there are no roots in the denominator or numerator.

**Example 1**. Rationalize the denominator.

<MathBlock>
$$
 \begin{align*}
 &\frac{4}{\sqrt{3}} \color{blue} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{3}}{\sqrt{3}} \\
 =& \frac{4\sqrt{3}}{(\sqrt{3})^2} \\
 =&\boxed{\frac{4\sqrt{3}}{3}}
 \end{align*}
$$
</MathBlock>

Basically, what we did is multiply the denominator or numerator from which we want to eliminate the square root by itself, so we can cancel the square root because it's a power of two.

**Example 2**. Rationalize the denominator.

<MathBlock>
$$
 \begin{align*}
 &\frac{\sqrt{x + h} + \sqrt{x - h}}{\sqrt{x + h} - \sqrt{x - h}} \color{blue} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{x + h} + \sqrt{x - h}}{\sqrt{x + h} + \sqrt{x - h}} \\
 =& \frac{(\sqrt{x + h} + \sqrt{x - h})^2}{(\sqrt{x + h})^2 - (\sqrt{x - h})^2}
 \end{align*}
$$
</MathBlock>

We want to rationalize the denominator in a way that, when we multiply, we end up with a difference of squares in the denominator.

Now we apply an algebraic identity to the numerator.

**Numerator**

<MathBlock>
$$
 \begin{align*}
 =&(\sqrt{x + h} + \sqrt{x - h})^2 \\
 =& (\sqrt{x + h})^2 + 2(\sqrt{x + h})(\sqrt{x - h}) + (\sqrt{x - h})^2 \\
 =& x + h + 2\sqrt{x^2 - h^2} + x - h \\
 =& \boxed{2x + 2\sqrt{x^2 - h^2}}
 \end{align*}
$$
</MathBlock>

**Denominator**

<MathBlock>
$$
 \begin{align*}
 =& (\sqrt{x + h})^2 - (\sqrt{x - h})^2 \\
 =& \cancel{x} + h \cancel{-x} + h \\
 =& \boxed{2h}
 \end{align*}
$$
</MathBlock>

So the final result would be

<MathBlock>
$$
 \boxed{\frac{2x + 2\sqrt{x^2 - h^2}}{2h}}
$$
</MathBlock>
